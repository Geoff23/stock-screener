Stock Screener 9.3 About this stock screener Graham's 7 criteria Timeline

1. Size

"...not less than $100 million of annual sales..."

The fourth edition of “The Intelligent Investor” was written half a century ago in the 1970s. Because of inflation, $100 million in annual sales then would not be much today. Fortunately, Jason Zweig suggested in the commentary that “the investor steer clear from stocks with a market value of less than $2 billion.” So, we will use market cap as the measure for size.

Stocks are usually divided into three categories: small-cap, mid-cap, and large-cap. Here are the definitions for each category:

  • Small-cap: less than $2 billion
  • Mid-cap: $2-10 billion
  • Large-cap: greater than $10 billion

To get stocks that have a market cap of at least $2 billion, select mid-cap and large-cap.

2. Financial Condition

"...current assets should be at least twice current liabilities - a so-called two-to-one current ratio. Also, long-term debt should not exceed the net current assets..."

To pass this test, you need to pass two requirements. Firstly, the current ratio must be at least 2. Secondly, long-term debt can not exceed net current assets. To find stocks that meet both requirements, enter 2 for current ratio and 1 for long-term debt to net current assets.

3. Earnings Stability

"Some earnings...in each of the past ten years."

This means that the company must be profitable in each of the last ten years. In other words, its net income must be positive for at least ten years.

4. Dividend Record

"Uninterrupted payments for at least the past 20 years."

This is similar to 3, but it evaluates dividends rather than earnings. The company must pay dividends continuously for 20 years or more.

5. Earnings Growth

A minimum increase of at least one-third in per-share earnings in the past ten years..."

This is calculated using 3-year averages at the beginning and end. For example, the earnings growth from 2011 to 2020 is the average EPS of 2018-2020 divided by that of 2009-2011. To get stocks with per-share earnings that grew 1/3 over the past ten years, enter 0.33.

6. Price to Earnings Ratio

"Current price should not be more than 15 times average earnings of the past three years."

PE ratio is calculated by dividing the market cap of a company by its total earnings. Here, we’re dividing by the average earnings of the past three years. A higher PE ratio means that the stock is more expensive, and a lower one means the opposite.

7. Price to Assets Ratio

"Current price should not be more than 1.5 times the book value last reported."

This means that the price/book ratio should be 1.5 at most. Book value is calculated by subtracting liabilities from tangible assets. Similar to a PE ratio, it is a way to measure whether a stock is overvalued or not.
